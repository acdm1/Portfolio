Abstract

This article may serve for some as an introduction to the usage of Indigenous ways of knowing around GIS technologies, to assist in the construction of future approaches to positive change. Pierce County has an interesting history where noise and policy are considered within the same context. Where urban design is the focus, soundscapes are understood by some as being considerably significant to the human condition and healthy development. After a brief historical overview of how noise as a nuisance has appeared throughout history then, Pierce County serves here as the location for observing how noise is experienced based on a variety of social determinants. By considering property values, geographic/scenic locations, and the ethnic identities of those occupying urban communities, we may learn more about ourselves, and thus better adapt to future calls for justice. Equity and justice arguments are often times conversations of how we perceive the users of spaces as either deserving or undeserving.Thus the aim of this project was to create a visually and sonically fulfilling experience, built around a multimedia map, which is accessible as both a recreational and educational tool.
Could an interactive policy map communicate themes pulled from environmental psychology, to better inform policy makers and readers as they attempt to recognize contemporary and future struggles around equity though? Critical scholarship would suggest that the answer is yes, but only by respectfully incorporating Indigenous knowledge and by utilizing approaches usually highlighted around queer studies disciplines. Thus the observations of Foucault are again relevant to those who engage with GIS technologies, as intersectionality and our perceptions of borders and legality are blurred by both our environmentally dependent psychological states as individuals, and our tendency as a collective to pathologize alternative lifestyles and cultures. The ability to recognize the intersections of geographic realities, technological advances, and digital conceptions are noted here as critical to efforts towards building a more reflexive discipline, and ultimately decolonizing research methodologies.

Keywords: Noise, Policy, Geography, Environmental Psychology, American Studies, Health & Society, Community Engagement
